• Blood : A fluid tissue that transports oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and wastes throughout the body.
  • Plasma : The liquid portion of blood that acts as the solvent, mainly composed of water.
  • Solvent : The substance that dissolves other substances in a solution, as learned in Chapter 2.
  • Solute : A substance dissolved in a solvent, such as glucose or salts in blood plasma.
  • Red blood cells : Cells that carry oxygen and carbon dioxide using hemoglobin.
  • Hemoglobin : A protein in red blood cells that binds to oxygen.
  • White blood cells : Cells that protect the body by fighting infections.
  • Neutrophils : White blood cells that act as the first line of defense against infection.
  • Lymphocytes : White blood cells that include T-cells and B-cells for immune response.
  • Platelets : Cell fragments that help blood clot and stop bleeding.
  • Coagulation : The process by which blood forms clots to prevent blood loss.
  • Colloidal suspension : A mixture where particles are suspended but not fully dissolved, like blood.
  • Particle theory : A theory stating that all matter is made of particles in constant motion.
  • Hematopoiesis : The process of blood cell formation in the bone marrow.
  • Bone marrow : The spongy tissue inside bones where blood cells are produced.
  • Vasodilation : The widening of blood vessels when blood is heated.
  • Vasoconstriction : The narrowing of blood vessels when blood is cooled.
  • Circulatory system : The system that transports blood throughout the body.
  • micrometre : 1000 nanometres
  • Diffusion : The movement of particles from high to low concentration, as learned in Chapter 1.
  • Concentration : The amount of solute dissolved in a given amount of solvent.
  • Solubility : The ability of a substance to dissolve in a solvent.
  • Blood test : A medical test that analyzes blood cells and dissolved substances.

  • Temperature regulation : The process by which blood helps maintain body temperature.
